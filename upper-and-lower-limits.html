Introduction
The concept of upper and lower limits (commonly denoted by lim sup⁡\limsuplimsup﻿ and lim inf⁡\liminfliminf﻿ respectively) shows up routinely when discussing the limiting behaviour of a sequence. For example, consider the Big O notation, O\mathcal{O}O﻿, defined as f(n)=O(g(n))f(n) = \mathcal{O}(g(n))f(n)=O(g(n))﻿ iff there exists a positive real number ccc﻿ and an integer NNN﻿ such that for every n∈Zn \in \Zn∈Z﻿, n>Nn > Nn>N﻿, we have ∣f(n)∣≤cg(n)|f(n)| \leq c g(n)∣f(n)∣≤cg(n)﻿. This can be succinctly written as
lim sup⁡n→∞∣f(n)∣g(n)<∞.\limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{|f(n)|}{g(n)} < \infty.n→∞limsup​g(n)∣f(n)∣​<∞.
In this post, I aim to expound on the concept of upper and lower limits so that the second formulation of Big O notation above becomes just as natural as the first one.

Upper and Lower Limits — A Reprise
There is an equivalent and often useful way to express upper and lower limits.
Definition 6: Let {sn}\{s_n\}{sn​}﻿ be a sequence of real numbers. We define the notation
sup⁡k≥nsk:=sup⁡{sk:k≥n}inf⁡k≥nsk:=inf⁡{sk:k≥n}.\begin{align*} \sup_{k \ge n} s_k &:= \sup \{s_k : k \ge n\} \\ \inf_{k \ge n} s_k &:= \inf \{s_k : k \ge n\}. \end{align*}k≥nsup​sk​k≥ninf​sk​​:=sup{sk​:k≥n}:=inf{sk​:k≥n}.​
We note that the sequence {sup⁡k≥nsk}n∈N\{\sup_{k \ge n} s_k\}_{n \in \N}{supk≥n​sk​}n∈N​﻿ is monotonically decreasing and the sequence {inf⁡k≥nsk}n∈N\{\inf_{k \ge n} s_k\}_{n \in \N}{infk≥n​sk​}n∈N​﻿ is monotonically increasing, and thus their limits exist in R‾\overline\RR﻿.
